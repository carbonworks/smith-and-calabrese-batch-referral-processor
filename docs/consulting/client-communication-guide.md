# Client Communication Guide

Communication guidance for each stage of the consulting engagement lifecycle. Referenced by the [Consulting Coach](/system/skills/consulting-coach.md) skill.

**Purpose**: Ensure every client interaction covers what needs to be said — especially for first-time clients where trust is still being built.

**Origin**: Created after the S&C Phase 1 engagement, where the contract was delivered without proactively explaining IP ownership, payment terms, or next steps. The follow-up message shouldn't have been necessary.

---

## Communication Calibration

Two factors shape how every client communication is written: **engagement history** and **relationship proximity**. Check both before drafting or reviewing any communication.

### Engagement History

Check the client profile frontmatter for `engagement-count`:
- **0 or absent** = first-time client — expand communication at every stage
- **1+** = returning client — standard communication is sufficient

Increment `engagement-count` at engagement closure.

First-time clients need more explanation because:
- They don't know how you work yet
- They can't fill in gaps from prior experience with you
- Trust is earned through transparency, not assumed

### Relationship Proximity

Check the primary contact's profile for `Relationship Proximity`. This determines the **register** (tone, formality, personal context) — not the **content** (you still cover the same checkpoints regardless of relationship).

| Proximity | Register | What changes | What stays the same |
|-----------|----------|--------------|---------------------|
| `close-friend` | Fully casual. Like texting a sibling. | Everything — tone, structure, length. Skip formalities entirely. | Core content: next steps, action items, key terms |
| `friend` | Familiar and natural. Personal references welcome, casual tone, no need to justify personal context (e.g., weekend plans). | Tone softens, structure loosens, personal transparency is natural (e.g., "still setting up payment processing" is fine). | All communication checkpoints still apply — cover next steps, reinforce key terms, surface action items. Just do it in a natural voice. |
| `warm-contact` | Friendly but professional. Light personal touches. | Slightly warmer greetings, can reference shared context. | Full professional structure and checkpoint coverage. |
| `professional` | Standard professional. Polished and clear. | Default mode — brand voice guidelines apply directly. | Everything per the stage-by-stage guidance below. |
| `cold-prospect` | Professional with extra context. Nothing assumed. | More explanation of who you are, how you work, why you're reaching out. | All checkpoints, plus additional credibility-building. |

**The key principle**: Relationship proximity adjusts the *register* — how you say it. It does not remove *content requirements* — what you need to say. A friend still needs to know what the next step is; you just tell them the way you'd tell a friend.

**If no proximity is set**, default to `professional`.

Reference: [Brand Rules — Professional Floor](/org/brand/brand-rules.md#professional-floor-context-calibrated) aligns content-type formality with this proximity scale.

## Stage 1: Client Intake

### Required Communication Actions

Send an **acknowledgment message** within 24 hours of first contact. Even if you're not ready to propose anything, acknowledge the conversation and set expectations for what happens next.

### Key Points to Cover

1. Thank them for the conversation / referral / outreach
2. Confirm your understanding of what they're looking for (in their words, not yours)
3. Explain your approach: listen first, then analyze, then propose options — not the other way around
4. State the next step and when they can expect to hear from you
5. Ask if there's anything urgent that can't wait for the normal timeline

### Email Structure

- **Subject**: Following up — [topic from conversation]
- **Opening**: Personal reference to the conversation (not generic)
- **Body**: Your understanding of their situation (2-3 sentences). What you'll do next and when.
- **Close**: Specific next step with timeframe. Open door for questions.

### First-Time Client Additions

- Briefly explain how you work: "I like to understand the full picture before recommending anything — so my first step is usually to ask questions, not pitch solutions."
- If referred, acknowledge the referrer by name
- Keep it short — this isn't a sales pitch, it's a human being responsive

### Common Mistakes (from S&C)

- Sending service offerings or pricing before understanding the client's specific problems
- Skipping the acknowledgment and jumping straight to discovery questions days later

## Stage 2: Discovery Prep

### Required Communication Actions

Send a **meeting confirmation** 1-2 days before the discovery meeting. If the meeting is informal (coffee, phone call), a brief text or email is still appropriate.

### Key Points to Cover

1. Confirm date, time, and location/format (in-person, phone, video)
2. Set expectations for the meeting: "I'll mostly be asking questions and listening"
3. Mention anything they should have handy (access to systems, examples of the problem, relevant documents)
4. Estimated duration
5. Reassure that there's no obligation — this is about understanding, not selling

### Email Structure

- **Subject**: Confirming [day] — [brief topic]
- **Opening**: Looking forward to the conversation
- **Body**: Logistics + what to expect + anything to prepare
- **Close**: "If anything changes, just let me know"

### First-Time Client Additions

- Explain what discovery means: "This is where I learn about your situation in detail so I can give you useful recommendations — not generic ones"
- If they're unfamiliar with consulting, normalize the process: "Most of my clients find this conversation is the most valuable part — it often clarifies things even before I do any work"

### Common Mistakes (from S&C)

- Not setting expectations for the meeting format, leading to uncertainty about what will happen
- Assuming the client knows what "discovery" means

## Stage 3: Discovery Debrief

### Required Communication Actions

Send a **summary email** within 48 hours of the discovery meeting. This demonstrates listening and gives the client a chance to correct misunderstandings before you invest time in solutions.

### Key Points to Cover

1. "Here's what I heard" — summarize the key problems/needs in their language
2. Confirm priorities: which problems matter most to them
3. State what you'll do next: "I'm going to research options for [X] and put together a few approaches for you"
4. Give a timeline for when they'll hear back
5. Ask if you missed anything or got something wrong
6. Note any immediate information needs (access to systems, sample files, etc.)

### Email Structure

- **Subject**: Summary from our conversation + next steps
- **Opening**: Thank them for their time and openness
- **Body**: Bulleted list of what you heard. Priorities as you understand them. What you'll do next.
- **Close**: Timeline for next touchpoint. Invitation to correct/add anything.

### First-Time Client Additions

- Be explicit about the process: "Here's what happens from here: I'll analyze the options, then come back with 2-3 approaches at different levels of investment so you can choose what fits"
- Explain that you won't disappear: "You'll hear from me by [date] with an update, even if I'm still working through the analysis"

### Common Mistakes (from S&C)

- Sending discovery questions 7 days after initial engagement without first summarizing what was already learned
- Not confirming priorities — assuming you know which problem matters most

## Stage 4: Solution Architecture

### Required Communication Actions

Send a **solution direction email** when you've analyzed the options — before writing a formal proposal. This is where you present thinking, not a finished document.

### Key Points to Cover

1. Reference back to their stated problems (show you listened)
2. Present 2-3 approaches with clear tradeoffs (not just "here's what I recommend")
3. Explain your pricing methodology: "I price based on the value and complexity of the work, not hours"
4. For each option: what it includes, what it costs, what they get, how long it takes
5. Be explicit about what's NOT included in each tier
6. State what happens next for whichever option they choose

### Email Structure

- **Subject**: Solution options for [problem area]
- **Opening**: Brief recap of what they asked for
- **Body**: Options presented clearly (table or structured list). Tradeoffs explained. Pricing with brief methodology note.
- **Close**: "I'm happy to walk through these on a call if that's easier" + next step

### First-Time Client Additions

- Explain the tiered approach: "I always present a few options so you can choose the level of investment that makes sense for you — there's no pressure to pick the biggest one"
- Proactively address pricing: "My pricing reflects the value of the outcome, not just the hours. I'm transparent about how I arrived at these numbers."
- If offering a first-client discount, frame it honestly: "Since this is our first project together, I've priced this as a first-engagement rate"

### Common Mistakes (from S&C)

- Sending solution options without explaining pricing methodology
- Not making tradeoffs explicit — client can't make a good decision without understanding what they're giving up at each tier

## Stage 5: Proposal Development

### Required Communication Actions

**Walk the client through the proposal** — live if possible (call or screen share), or via a structured email with section-by-section explanation. Never send a proposal cold.

### Key Points to Cover

1. This proposal reflects what you've discussed — no surprises
2. Walk through each section: situation → objectives → methodology → timing → terms
3. Explain the three tiers and help them think about which fits
4. Clarify any terms that might be unfamiliar (retainer, T&M, deliverables)
5. Explain what acceptance looks like: "If this looks right, here's how we move forward"
6. Set a decision timeline: "Take whatever time you need — I'll follow up in [X days] if I haven't heard back"

### Email Structure

- **Subject**: Proposal for [project name] — for your review
- **Opening**: "This captures everything we've discussed. No surprises — just structure around what we've already agreed on."
- **Body**: Brief walkthrough of key sections. Highlight anything that's new or different from prior conversations. Note the three tiers and what you'd recommend given their situation.
- **Close**: Next step (sign, discuss, or schedule a walkthrough). Decision timeline.

### First-Time Client Additions

- Offer a live walkthrough: "Since this is our first time working together, I'd love to walk you through this on a quick call so you can ask questions as we go"
- Explain the proposal format: "This is structured as a formal proposal — it covers the situation as I understand it, what we'd accomplish, how we'd do it, and the investment"
- Reassure about flexibility: "Nothing here is carved in stone. If something doesn't feel right, let's talk about it."

### Common Mistakes (from S&C)

- Sending a proposal without a walkthrough or explanation of the structure
- Assuming the client knows what each section means

## Stage 6: Contract Delivery

**This is the most critical communication stage.** A contract is a formal document that can feel intimidating, especially for a first-time client. The cover email must explain everything the client needs to understand — don't make them figure it out from legal language.

### Required Communication Actions

Send a **cover email with the contract** that explains every key term in plain language. The contract itself has plain-language summaries, but the email should independently cover the essentials.

### Key Points to Cover

1. **What this document is**: "This is a services agreement for [project]. It puts into writing what we've discussed."
2. **Scope summary**: What you'll do, what they'll provide, in 2-3 sentences
3. **Payment terms**: Amount, when it's due, how to pay — in plain language, not legalese
4. **IP ownership**: Who owns what when it's done. Use plain language: "Everything I build for you belongs to you. I retain no rights to your data or custom work."
5. **What happens after signing**: The concrete next steps — "Once you sign, here's what happens: [step 1], [step 2], [step 3]"
6. **Timeline**: When work starts, key milestones, estimated completion
7. **Warranty/support**: What happens after delivery — is there a support period? What does it cover?
8. **Communication cadence**: How often you'll be in touch during the project. "You'll hear from me at least [frequency] with updates."
9. **How to sign**: Exact mechanics (PandaDoc, DocuSign, wet signature, etc.)
10. **Questions welcome**: "Read through it, and if anything is unclear or doesn't feel right, let's talk before you sign"

### Email Structure

- **Subject**: Services agreement for [project name] — for your review
- **Opening**: "Attached is the services agreement for [project]. This puts our conversation into a formal structure — no surprises."
- **Body**: Walk through the 10 key points above in natural language. Keep each point to 1-2 sentences. Use bold headers or a numbered list for scannability.
- **Close**: How to sign + timeline expectations + open door for questions

### First-Time Client Additions

- **Explain the contract's purpose**: "This protects both of us — it makes sure we're on the same page about what I'll deliver, what it costs, and what happens if something unexpected comes up."
- **Proactively address common concerns**:
  - IP: "Section [X] covers intellectual property. In plain terms: you own everything I create for you."
  - Liability: "Section [X] limits liability — this is standard for professional services. It means [plain explanation]."
  - Feasibility: If applicable: "There's a feasibility clause — if during the work I discover the approach won't work, I'll tell you immediately rather than charging for something that can't deliver."
- **Normalize questions**: "Contracts can feel formal, but this is really just a written version of what we've already agreed to. If anything reads differently from what you expected, that's my mistake in drafting — let me know and we'll fix it."

### Common Mistakes (from S&C)

- **Sending the contract with no explanation of key terms** — the client shouldn't have to parse legal language to understand what they're agreeing to. This was the primary failure in the S&C Phase 1 delivery.
- Not explaining IP ownership proactively — even when the contract says "client owns everything," the client may not find or understand that section
- Not explaining what happens after signing — the client is left wondering "now what?"
- Not establishing communication cadence — the client doesn't know when they'll hear from you or how the project will feel day-to-day
- Assuming the contract speaks for itself — it doesn't, especially for first-time clients

## Stage 7: Engagement Check-in

### Required Communication Actions

Send **regular status updates** at the cadence established in the kickoff. Use the RAG (Red/Amber/Green) status format for consistency.

### Key Points to Cover

1. Overall status: Green (on track), Amber (minor issues), or Red (needs attention)
2. What was accomplished since last update
3. What's planned for the next period
4. Any blockers or risks — and what you're doing about them
5. Decisions needed from the client (with deadlines)
6. Budget/hours status if applicable

### Email Structure

- **Subject**: [Project name] — Status update [date]
- **Opening**: Overall status in one line: "Everything is on track" or "One item needs your attention"
- **Body**: RAG status details. Accomplishments. Next steps. Blockers/decisions needed.
- **Close**: Next update date. Any action items for the client.

### First-Time Client Additions

- **On the first update**: Confirm the communication cadence: "I'll send updates like this every [frequency]. If you ever want more or less detail, just let me know."
- Explain the RAG format: "Green means on track, amber means there's something to watch, red means we need to talk. Most updates should be green."
- Be slightly more detailed than you would for a returning client — they don't have a baseline for what "normal" looks like in your engagements

### Common Mistakes (from S&C)

- Not establishing a regular cadence from the start — the client wonders if work is happening
- Inconsistent update format — the client doesn't know what to expect
- Burying decisions needed in the middle of a long update — put action items prominently

## Stage 8: Engagement Closure

### Required Communication Actions

Send a **delivery summary email** when the engagement is complete. This isn't just "here are the files" — it's a structured handoff that ensures the client knows what they got, how to use it, and what comes next.

### Key Points to Cover

1. **What was delivered**: Complete list of deliverables with brief descriptions
2. **How to use it**: Quick-start guidance or reference to documentation you provided
3. **Where things live**: File locations, access credentials, system URLs
4. **Warranty/support period**: When it starts, what it covers, how to reach you
5. **What's NOT covered**: Boundaries of the warranty — changes, enhancements, etc.
6. **Feedback request**: "How did this go? What could I do better next time?"
7. **Future work**: If there are natural next phases, mention them lightly — not as a sales pitch, but as context: "When you're ready to tackle [problem 2], I'm here."

### Email Structure

- **Subject**: [Project name] — Delivery complete + next steps
- **Opening**: "The [project] is complete. Here's a summary of what was delivered and how to use it."
- **Body**: Deliverables list. Usage guidance. Support/warranty info. Feedback request.
- **Close**: Warm close. Light mention of future work if applicable. Thank them.

### First-Time Client Additions

- Be more detailed on the "how to use it" section — don't assume they'll figure it out
- Explicitly state the relationship going forward: "Even though this project is wrapping up, you can always reach out if questions come up. I want this to work well for you long-term."
- If the engagement went well, this is the right time to ask: "Would you be comfortable referring me to others in your network who might have similar challenges?"

### Common Mistakes (from S&C)

- Delivering files without explaining how to use them
- Not establishing what happens after delivery — client wonders if you've disappeared
- Missing the window for feedback and referral requests — do it while the experience is fresh

## General Principles

### Voice

All client communication should sound like a knowledgeable neighbor — someone who's approachable, competent, and genuinely trying to help. Not a vendor pitching. Not a lawyer covering liability. A person who explains things clearly because they respect the other person's time and intelligence.

Reference: [Carbon Works Brand Guidelines](/org/brand/carbon-works-brand-guidelines.md)

### Timing

- Acknowledge within 24 hours
- Status updates on the cadence you committed to (never late)
- If you'll miss a self-imposed deadline, communicate before the deadline, not after
- Silence is never acceptable — even a "still working on it, update coming [date]" is better than nothing

### Transparency

- If you don't know something, say so
- If something is going wrong, surface it early
- If pricing methodology is unusual, explain it
- If a contract term might be confusing, explain it before they have to ask

### First-Time Client Multiplier

Every communication principle matters more with first-time clients. They're forming their impression of how you work. The extra 5 minutes spent explaining something proactively saves hours of confusion, follow-up emails, and trust repair later.

## Appendix: S&C Lessons Learned

These lessons from the Smith & Calabrese Phase 1 engagement directly informed this guide:

1. **Contract sent with no explanation of key terms** — IP ownership, payment terms, and next steps were all in the contract but not explained in the delivery email. Required a follow-up message to cover what should have been in the original communication.

2. **Discovery questions came 7 days after initial engagement** — The gap between first contact and substantive follow-up was too long, and the follow-up jumped to questions without summarizing what was already known.

3. **No kickoff communication establishing cadence** — The client didn't know when to expect updates or how the engagement would feel day-to-day.

4. **No proactive explanation of pricing methodology** — Pricing was presented without context for how it was derived, leaving the client to accept it on faith.

5. **Service offerings sent before discovery was complete** — Generic service catalog was shared before understanding the client's specific problems, which felt like a pitch rather than a conversation.

These weren't catastrophic errors — the engagement landed and the relationship is strong. But they created unnecessary friction that better communication habits would have prevented.
